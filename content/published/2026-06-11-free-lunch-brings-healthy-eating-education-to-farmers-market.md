# Free Lunch Brings Healthy Eating Education to Farmers Market

**TL;DR:** Chesterfield County Public Schools and local growers partner to offer free lunches and hands-on wellness education to children at the weekly farmers market.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** Chesterfield County Public Schools Food and Nutrition Services, local farmers market vendors, Virginia Cooperative Extension, and community partners
- **What:** Free lunch program combining meals with hands-on nutrition and wellness activities for children
- **When:** Wednesdays, May 6 through September 30, 2026 (lunch served 11 a.m.–1:30 p.m.)
- **Where:** Chesterfield County Farmers Market, 6701 Mimms Loop, Chesterfield, VA 23832

## The story

Chesterfield County Public Schools has launched an expanded summer meal program at the Chesterfield County Farmers Market that combines free lunches for children with hands-on education about nutrition, local agriculture, and healthy eating. Every Wednesday from May 6 through September 30, 2026, children 18 and under can receive complimentary meals while participating in weekly wellness activities and learning opportunities.

The program operates through a direct partnership between the school district's Food and Nutrition Services and local vendors already present at the market. Assistant Director Casey Dickinson works with farmers to understand seasonal crop availability, then develops weekly menus using resources from thelunchbox.org and USDA Team Nutrition recipes. Meals are prepared in the district's central kitchen and transported to the market site in a hot/cold holding van, ensuring fresh, scratch-made options like Chicken Caesar wraps, beef tacos, and seasonal fruit offerings.

Beyond meal service, the program emphasizes experiential learning. Weekly rotating activities connect children directly to food systems and wellness. Past programs have included hands-on visits from local beekeepers who brought hives and taught children about honey production, demonstrations from agricultural experts, and interactive educational resources from nonprofit partners. These activities transform the farmers market into a living classroom where children learn farm-to-table concepts while enjoying meals prepared from locally sourced ingredients.

The initiative has proven popular with the community. In its first year of expanded operations, the program served 475 meals across seven service days. By the second year, participation surged to 930 meals in just five service days. The farmers market itself has seen increased overall attendance since the lunch program began—average weekly participation rose from 737 people before the program to 981 people once the district began serving free meals each Wednesday.

## The case for

The program directly addresses food access and nutrition education for families with limited resources. Free meals remove barriers to healthy eating during summer months when school meals end, while integrated learning activities—from beekeeping to farm partnerships—teach children practical knowledge about where food comes from. The initiative strengthens local food systems by increasing demand at the farmers market, benefiting local growers and vendors. Community participation has increased sharply, broadening the market's reach beyond traditional shoppers and supporting both food equity and local agriculture simultaneously.

## The case against

Expanding meal programs requires school district budget allocation and staffing in a period of competing fiscal priorities. Transportation of prepared meals to an off-site location adds operational complexity and holds true for meal safety compliance under USDA guidelines. While activities rotate weekly, the program's explicit nutrition education component remains modest compared to formal classroom instruction, and sustained engagement may vary by child and season. Some argue that resources might achieve greater impact if directed toward year-round food access programs or school-based nutrition curricula.

**Why it matters:** For families in Chesterfield County, the program bridges a critical summer meal gap while teaching children about healthy eating and local food systems. For local farmers and vendors, increased market foot traffic and direct sales to the school district strengthen economic viability. The model demonstrates how public schools can be anchors for community health and local economic development.
