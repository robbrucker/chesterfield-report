# Chesterfield Parents Push Schools to Cut Screen Time

**TL;DR:** A growing movement of more than 2,500 parents across Central Virginia is pushing Chesterfield County Public Schools to reduce classroom screen time and shift back toward paper-based instruction for younger students.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** More than 2,500 Central Virginia parents, led by Amber Rhodes (former teacher, CCPS mother); Chesterfield County Public Schools; Superintendent John Murray
- **What:** Parents calling for reduced device use in classrooms, device removal from youngest grades, screen time limits, and more paper-based instruction
- **When:** Campaign peak June 2026; survey deadline June 12, 2026; next board meeting Aug. 11
- **Where:** Chesterfield County, Virginia

## The story

A growing movement of parents across Central Virginia is pushing back against heavy reliance on digital devices in public school classrooms, with Chesterfield County emerging as a focal point for the debate. More than 2,500 parents have signed petitions calling on local school districts—including Chesterfield, Henrico, and Richmond—to recalibrate how technology is used in instruction. The petitions outline eight specific requests: establishing screen-time limits, removing devices from the youngest elementary grades, encouraging paper-based learning, and blocking non-educational platforms on school-issued devices.

Leading the effort in Chesterfield County is Amber Rhodes, a former teacher and mother of three children in the district. Rhodes created a petition that gathered over 1,000 signatures and presented it—along with 71 written comments from concerned parents—directly to Superintendent John Murray in a meeting this spring. "We're not trying to take technology away," Rhodes emphasized, "we're trying to just be more intentional about the way that we use technology." The movement reflects a broader national conversation about the role of devices in young children's learning and well-being.

Currently, Chesterfield County Public Schools provides Chromebooks in all grades, with a more intensive rollout in grades 6-12, where students are assigned individual devices that go home nightly. In elementary schools, devices are available but schools have discretion in determining when and how they're used in the classroom. This spring, the district surveyed parents, teachers, and administrators through Project Tomorrow's Speak Up initiative to gather feedback on digital learning and technology use. Superintendent Murray indicated that the district is committed to balance and that a work group is actively discussing how to achieve it in all classrooms. Murray met with parent advocates and left them "optimistic that some changes could be implemented before the start of the next school year," suggesting the district is taking the concerns seriously.

The Chesterfield push comes as similar debates are unfolding statewide. Virginia Beach's School Board recently approved a policy barring Chromebook use in pre-K and kindergarten, with limited access for first graders and restricted take-home privileges for second graders. These moves reflect a national trend toward reassessing screen time in early elementary classrooms, where educators and parents increasingly question whether devices enhance or hinder learning in foundational years.

## The case for

**Potential Benefits of Reduced Screen Time:**
Limiting device use, especially in younger grades, can improve focus, fine motor skill development, and reduce eye strain. Paper-based learning encourages deeper engagement with material, supports better note-taking and retention, and fosters direct interaction between students and teachers. Removing Chromebooks from elementary classrooms aligns with developmental research suggesting young children benefit from hands-on, tactile learning. Additionally, stricter device policies could reduce distractions from non-educational apps and platforms during instruction, allowing teachers to reclaim class time for direct instruction and interpersonal learning.

## The case against

**Potential Challenges of Device Removal:**
Chromebooks enable differentiated instruction, allowing teachers to quickly customize content for diverse learners and provide immediate formative assessment. Digital tools expand access to resources and support students with disabilities through adaptive technology. Removing devices mid-rollout could disrupt lesson planning and leave schools with underutilized hardware investments—Chesterfield recently leased new Chromebooks for a four-year term. For families without home internet or computers, school-issued devices bridge equity gaps. Additionally, technology skills are increasingly essential; removing devices entirely deprives students of necessary digital literacy practice. A blanket policy may be too broad—the real challenge is smarter implementation (intentional use, time limits, curriculum alignment) rather than elimination.

**Why it matters:** The outcome of this debate will shape how Chesterfield students spend their instructional time and what learning tools they rely on over the next several years. Parents' concerns about screen time reflect legitimate questions about student well-being and academic effectiveness, while the district's commitment to finding balance suggests changes are coming—decisions made in the coming months could serve as a template for other Virginia districts grappling with the same tension.
