# Chesterfield supervisors approve $2.3B budget for fiscal 2027

**TL;DR:** Chesterfield County supervisors unanimously approved a $2.3 billion budget for fiscal year 2027, featuring a 10-cent car tax cut, 5% employee raises, and record school funding.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** Chesterfield County Board of Supervisors
- **What:** Approved $2.3 billion fiscal 2027 budget with car tax reduction and school funding increases
- **When:** April 8, 2026
- **Where:** Chesterfield County, Virginia

## The story

Chesterfield County supervisors unanimously approved a $2.3 billion operating budget for fiscal year 2027, which begins July 1, 2026. The general fund totals $1.1 billion, a 4.7% increase from the previous fiscal year. The board voted on the budget April 8, 2026, following public hearings held March 25.

The budget reflects three core county priorities: education, public safety, and infrastructure. About 83% of the general fund is allocated toward these areas. The school system receives $1.1 billion—the largest portion of the budget—plus an additional $15 million that funds a 5% pay raise for school employees. The starting teacher salary in Chesterfield will rise from $55,047 to $57,277. Public safety receives $3.7 million in funding to improve staffing competitiveness and equipment for police, fire, EMS, and the sheriff's office.

The county reduced the personal property tax rate by 10 cents to $3.25 per $100 of assessed value—the lowest rate in the region—while maintaining the real estate tax rate at 89 cents per $100. The budget also dedicates $5 million to expanded tax relief for disabled veterans and seniors. County budget director Gerard Durkin emphasized that "99.8% of this budget is dedicated to existing services," with only $2.7 million designated for new operating programs.

Major infrastructure investments include $689 million toward the county's fourth water source at the Appomattox River Basin, slated to come online within a decade, and $47.1 million allocated over five years for facility modernization. The county also invested $2.7 million in cybersecurity and digital infrastructure to strengthen data protection and technology systems.

## The case for

The budget prioritizes essential services while managing growth: schools receive record funding with competitive teacher pay raises to recruit talent; public safety gets resources to maintain staffing levels; car tax cuts provide relief to residents; and long-term infrastructure investments like the fourth water source address future capacity needs as the county continues growing.

## The case against

With 99.8% of the budget committed to existing services, there is minimal funding ($2.7 million) for new or expanded programs, limiting the county's ability to launch new initiatives or respond to emerging community needs. The emphasis on long-term projects like the water source (a decade away) may delay near-term solutions, and relying heavily on existing service levels could stretch resources if demand increases faster than anticipated.

**Why it matters:** The budget directly affects resident taxes (car tax cuts), school quality and teacher recruitment (5% raises), emergency response capabilities (public safety funding), and long-term quality of life (water infrastructure and facility modernization). It sets spending priorities that shape county services for the coming fiscal year as Chesterfield continues to grow.
