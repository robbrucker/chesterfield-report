# Vandalism Suspect Ordered for Mental Health Evaluation

**TL;DR:** A Midlothian resident accused of damaging four county buildings, including the courthouse, in a May vandalism spree has been ordered to undergo mental health evaluation.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** Andrew Stillerman, 39, Midlothian resident
- **What:** Accused of felony and misdemeanor destruction of property across four buildings
- **When:** May 6, 2026, approximately 11:30 p.m.
- **Where:** Chesterfield County, Virginia

## The story

Andrew Stillerman, 39, of the 700 block of Abbey Drive Circle in Midlothian, was arrested and charged with felony and misdemeanor destruction of property after a late-night vandalism spree on May 6, 2026. Around 11:30 p.m., Stillerman allegedly targeted four buildings across Chesterfield County: a Sheetz gas station off Courthouse and Iron Bridge Roads, where he stole a flag; the Chesterfield County main courthouse, where he removed his pants before throwing a brick through the front doors; the Chesterfield County Central Library, where he smashed a door with a large rock; and First Watch restaurant, where he broke windows.

Sheriff Karl S. Leonard told reporters that the vandalism appeared to stem from random acts by someone experiencing mental health struggles rather than targeted incidents. Leonard, who has led the Sheriff's Office since 2014 and oversees a force serving over 320,000 residents, emphasized that the intersection of mental illness and criminal charges "compounds an issue" within the system. The court subsequently ordered Stillerman to undergo a comprehensive mental health evaluation as part of the legal proceedings.

Chesterfield County has established treatment courts and mental health support services to address cases involving defendants with mental health concerns. The county operates a Recovery Court since 2000 and a Veteran Treatment Docket since December 2023, both in cooperation with the judiciary, Commonwealth's Attorney's Office, and community corrections. Additionally, Chesterfield Mental Health Support Services provides evaluations and treatment referrals by court order.

Stillerman's next status hearing is scheduled for September 4, 2026. His case reflects broader challenges many jurisdictions face in balancing criminal accountability with appropriate mental health intervention for defendants experiencing psychiatric crises.

## The case for

Court-ordered mental health evaluation can facilitate early intervention and appropriate treatment rather than incarceration alone, potentially addressing root causes of behavior and reducing recidivism. When effective, linking defendants with mental health services may result in better public safety outcomes and cost savings compared to criminal prosecution without therapeutic support.

## The case against

Mental health evaluations and treatment diversion programs require significant resources and may not guarantee public safety if adequate follow-up care and oversight are unavailable. Some argue that property damage warrants accountability independent of mental health status, and that emphasis on evaluation without balancing consequences may undermine deterrence or appear to excuse harmful behavior.

**Why it matters:** This case highlights how Chesterfield County's courts and law enforcement address criminal conduct tied to mental health crises. For residents, it illustrates both the community impact of vandalism targeting public facilities and the county's capacity to redirect cases involving mental illness toward evaluation and treatment rather than purely punitive measures.
