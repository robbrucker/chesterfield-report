# Chesterfield accused of destroying public records in lawsuit

**TL;DR:** Chesterfield County faces sanctions and litigation cost demands after allegedly destroying public records at the center of a nearly four-year-old lawsuit filed by a local woman who criticized police handling of a child abuse report.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** Charlotte Carter (plaintiff), Chesterfield County Police Department, County Attorney's office
- **What:** Record destruction allegations in public information lawsuit; plaintiff seeking sanctions and cost reimbursement
- **When:** FOIA request filed 2022; lawsuit filed Nov 2022; sanctions motion filed March 2026; trial arguments scheduled June 2026
- **Where:** Chesterfield County, Virginia

## The story

Charlotte Carter, a Chesterfield County nurse, filed a Freedom of Information Act request in late 2022 seeking incident records related to her arrests, including body camera footage, 911 calls, police radio recordings, and incident reports. The county responded with a $24,000 cost estimate to review and produce the files. Carter challenged the fee as unreasonable and filed a lawsuit in November 2022.

Carter's requests for public records came after she reported suspected child abuse to authorities and later posted a viral TikTok video criticizing the police department's handling of her report and questioning an officer's traffic stop patterns. Following the video, which drew nearly 5 million views, she was arrested multiple times by the Chesterfield County Police Department. All charges were later dismissed, nolle prossed, or resulted in acquittals.

Nearly four years into the lawsuit, Carter's attorney Joy Ramsingh discovered that Chesterfield County had destroyed much of the records in question. Court filings reveal that police radio recordings—including communications one hour before and after each of Carter's arrests—were deleted between late 2022 and early 2023 through the county's automated record deletion system, which purges emergency radio recordings after six months. Critically, many of these deletions occurred *after* Carter filed her FOIA petition but without disclosure to Carter or her counsel. In March 2026, Ramsingh filed a motion requesting sanctions against Chesterfield County and county attorney Julie Seyfarth, seeking full reimbursement of litigation costs and attorney fees accumulated over nearly four years.

The county's response argues that Carter's FOIA request was deemed withdrawn when she did not pay the cost estimate, that police radio transmissions were never retrievable records, and that Chesterfield had no legal obligation to preserve the files once the request was withdrawn. Trial arguments on the sanctions motion are scheduled for June 2026. The case raises questions about government transparency, document retention practices, and whether agencies must preserve records when FOIA requests are disputed.

## The case for

From the county's perspective, the FOIA request was withdrawn when the requester declined to pay the processing fee—a standard practice in public records law. The county argues it had no duty to preserve records for a withdrawn request, and that police radio systems are designed with automatic deletion functions for operational and storage efficiency. The county also contends that without a valid, active FOIA request, records retention obligations do not apply, and that Carter's failure to pay the estimate terminated the request.

## The case against

Carter's counsel argues the county should have preserved records once a FOIA lawsuit was filed, since litigation automatically creates a legal hold requirement. The destruction of records at the center of active litigation—especially without disclosure to the opposing party—suggests improper conduct and potential destruction of evidence. The timing is particularly damaging: records were deleted after the FOIA petition was filed but before the county disclosed that they no longer existed. Additionally, the $24,000 fee estimate itself may have been unreasonably high and used as a practical barrier to access, raising fairness concerns about FOIA compliance.

**Why it matters:** The case highlights how Chesterfield County residents exercise government transparency rights and whether the county properly preserves documents central to public information disputes. If the court finds the county liable for destroying records during litigation, it could establish stricter record retention requirements for county agencies and result in significant costs to taxpayers. The outcome may also influence how police departments manage automated record deletion systems and their responsibilities during FOIA disputes.
