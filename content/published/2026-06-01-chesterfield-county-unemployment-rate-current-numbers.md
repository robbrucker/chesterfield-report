# Chesterfield County Unemployment Rate: Current Numbers

**TL;DR:** Chesterfield County's unemployment rate stood at 3.3% in April 2026, up slightly from 2.9% a year earlier, reflecting moderate growth in what remains a strong regional job market.

## Quick facts

- **Who:** Chesterfield County residents and labor force
- **What:** Unemployment rate at 3.3% as of April 2026; county economy includes 147,000+ jobs and $22.4 billion in economic activity
- **When:** April 2026
- **Where:** Chesterfield County, Virginia (81st of 133 Virginia counties for unemployment)

## The story

Chesterfield County's unemployment rate reached 3.3% in April 2026, representing a slight uptick from 2.9% in April 2025—an increase of 0.4 percentage points year-over-year, according to USAFacts data grounded in Bureau of Labor Statistics figures. While this marks a modest rise, Chesterfield's rate remains competitive within Virginia's landscape. The county ranks 81st out of 133 Virginia counties for unemployment, with state rates ranging from 2.6% to 7.3% during the same period. For context, Virginia's overall unemployment rate stood at 3.8% in April 2026.

The county's job market reflects significant recent investment and economic diversification. Chesterfield boasts over 147,000 jobs—an all-time high—with an average weekly wage exceeding $1,200, also at record levels according to U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The county's $22.4 billion local economy is anchored by a diversified mix of industries: healthcare, finance, government, and technology remain major sectors, with companies like Dominion Energy, Capital One, and the Virginia Commonwealth University Health System employing thousands in the greater Richmond region.

Recent major capital investments underscore the county's economic momentum. Commonwealth Fusion Systems announced plans to build the world's first grid-scale commercial fusion power plant in Chesterfield—a nearly $3 billion commitment. Other significant projects include Chirisa Technology Parks (data centers, $2 billion), the LEGO Group's precision manufacturing facility ($1 billion), Topsoe's clean energy technology campus ($400 million), and Plenty Unlimited's vertical indoor farming complex ($300 million). In 2024 alone, Chesterfield attracted $2.8 billion in new investments and generated nearly 1,200 new jobs. Over the past six years, announced investment for new commercial and industrial projects has surpassed $11.3 billion.

More than 10,300 companies operate in Chesterfield County, reflecting a robust business ecosystem. While the slight year-over-year increase in unemployment may warrant monitoring, the county's strong job creation, record employment levels, and continuing major capital inflows suggest underlying economic resilience. Residents seeking detailed labor market data and workforce trends can consult Virginia Works, the official state source for labor market information.

## The case for

Chesterfield's unemployment rate remains low by national and state standards, with a $22.4 billion economy, 147,000+ jobs at all-time highs, record average wages exceeding $1,200 per week, and over $11 billion in announced investment over six years. Major commitments from Commonwealth Fusion Systems ($3 billion), LEGO ($1 billion), and others signal sustained employer confidence in the county's workforce and infrastructure, positioning residents well for employment growth.

## The case against

The year-over-year increase of 0.4 percentage points—from 2.9% to 3.3%—signals a modest slowdown in hiring momentum, suggesting employers may be moderating growth after a period of rapid expansion. While still healthy by broader standards, the rising rate warrants attention to industry-specific employment trends and potential wage pressures as the county competes nationally for talent in emerging sectors like fusion energy and advanced manufacturing.

**Why it matters:** The unemployment rate is a key indicator of regional economic health and job availability. A 3.3% unemployment rate reflects a tight labor market with relatively strong employment opportunities, though the recent uptick suggests a normalization after rapid hiring. Understanding these trends helps residents assess their own job prospects and informs county economic development priorities.
